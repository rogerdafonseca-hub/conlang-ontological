# 🔊 PHONOLOGY — Sound System

**Universal Ontological Language**

_A phonemic inventory designed for cross-linguistic accessibility and systematic elegance._

---

## 🎯 Introduction

The phonology of this language is built on a simple principle: **use sounds that are common across the world's languages**.

By selecting the most frequent consonants and vowels found in diverse language families, we ensure that:

- ✅ Most learners already know how to produce these sounds
- ✅ No sound system is privileged over others
- ✅ The language is physically easy to pronounce
- ✅ The inventory is small enough to learn quickly (30 phonemes total)

**Total phonemes:** 30

- **25 consonants** (from IPA Top 25 most common)
- **5 primary vowels** (universal vowel triangle)
- **4 additional vowels** (for special grammatical/contextual uses)

---

## 🧭 Design Principles

### **1. Cross-Linguistic Frequency**

Every consonant in this language appears in **50-95% of the world's languages**.

**Source:** PHOIBLE Database (3,000+ languages analyzed)

**Selection criteria:**

- Present in at least 50% of languages globally
- Found across multiple language families (not just Indo-European)
- Easy to articulate (no extreme sounds like clicks or ejectives)
- Distinctive (minimal overlap in articulation)

### **2. Phonetic Balance**

The inventory includes:

- **Plosives** (p, t, k, b, d, g) — voiceless and voiced pairs
- **Nasals** (m, n, ɲ, ŋ) — bilabial, alveolar, palatal, velar
- **Fricatives** (f, v, s, z, ʃ, ʒ, x, h) — varying places of articulation
- **Affricates** (tʃ, dʒ, ts) — combined articulations
- **Liquids** (l, r) — lateral and rhotic
- **Approximants** (w, j) — semivowels

**Result:** A balanced, complete system covering all major manners of articulation.

### **3. Avoiding Rare Sounds**

**Sounds deliberately excluded:**

- **/θ, ð/** (dental fricatives) — only in ~10% of languages (English, Greek, Arabic)
- **/ʔ/** (glottal stop) — while common, it's often allophonic rather than phonemic
- **/q/** (uvular plosive) — limited to specific regions
- **Clicks, ejectives, implosives** — too exotic for a universal language

### **Articulatory Descriptions**

**For learners who need guidance on HOW to produce each sound:**

#### **Plosives (Stop Consonants)**

**How to produce:**  
Completely block airflow, then release suddenly.

- **/p/** — Press lips together, release without voice (like blowing out a candle)
- **/b/** — Same as /p/ but with voice (vocal cords vibrate)
- **/t/** — Tongue tip touches alveolar ridge (behind upper teeth), release
- **/d/** — Same as /t/ but voiced
- **/k/** — Back of tongue touches soft palate (roof of mouth), release
- **/g/** — Same as /k/ but voiced

#### **Nasals**

**How to produce:**  
Block oral airflow, let air escape through nose.

- **/m/** — Lips together, air through nose (hum with lips closed)
- **/n/** — Tongue tip at alveolar ridge, air through nose
- **/ɲ/** — Middle of tongue touches hard palate (like "ny" in "canyon")
- **/ŋ/** — Back of tongue touches soft palate (like "ng" in "sing")

#### **Fricatives**

**How to produce:**  
Narrow airflow channel, creating friction noise.

- **/f/** — Upper teeth touch lower lip, blow air (voiceless)
- **/v/** — Same as /f/ but voiced
- **/s/** — Tongue creates narrow channel at alveolar ridge (snake sound)
- **/z/** — Same as /s/ but voiced
- **/ʃ/** — Tongue farther back than /s/, lips slightly rounded (like "sh" in "shush")
- **/ʒ/** — Same as /ʃ/ but voiced (like "s" in "measure")
- **/x/** — Back of tongue near soft palate, rough friction (like German "Bach" or Spanish "jamón")
- **/h/** — Open throat, gentle breath (like breathing on glasses to clean them)

#### **Affricates**

**How to produce:**  
Start as plosive, release as fricative (combined sound).

- **/tʃ/** — Start with /t/, release as /ʃ/ (like "ch" in "church")
- **/dʒ/** — Same as /tʃ/ but voiced (like "j" in "judge")
- **/ts/** — Start with /t/, release as /s/ (like "ts" in "cats")

#### **Liquids and Approximants**

- **/l/** — Tongue tip touches alveolar ridge, air escapes around sides
- **/r/** — **Flexible!** Can be trilled [r], tapped [ɾ], or approximant [ɹ] — all acceptable
- **/w/** — Round lips, raise back of tongue toward velum (like "w" in "water")
- **/j/** — Raise middle of tongue toward hard palate (like "y" in "yes")

## 🧩 Phonotactics

**Syllable structure rules — what combinations are allowed.**

### **Core Principle: CV Structure**

The language **strongly prefers** CV (Consonant + Vowel) syllables.

**Allowed syllable types:**

|Type|Structure|Examples|Frequency|
|---|---|---|---|
|**V**|Vowel only|`a`, `i`, `o`|Rare (word-initial only)|
|**CV**|Consonant + Vowel|`pa`, `ta`, `ka`|**Primary (90%)**|
|**CCV**|Cons + Cons + Vowel|`pra`, `bla`, `ska`|Limited (borrowed words)|

**NOT allowed:**

- ❌ CVC syllables in native words (`pat`, `tok`)
- ❌ Complex codas (`aks`, `ints`)
- ❌ Consonant clusters at end of words

**Why CV?**

1. **Universal** — All languages have CV syllables
2. **Easy** — Simple to pronounce, no ambiguity
3. **Clear** — Each syllable boundary is obvious
4. **Systematic** — Supports the grammatical structure (CV particles, VCV clusters)

---

### **Word Structure Patterns**

#### **Grammar Particles (CV)**

```
pa, pe, pi, po, pu
ta, te, ti, to, tu
ka, ke, ki, ko, ku
```

#### **Extended Grammar (VCV)**

```
apa, epa, ipa, opa, upa
aba, ebe, ibi, obo, ubu
```

#### **Nominal Lexicon (CV+CV+...)**

```
kafe  (coffee)
pitsa (pizza)
telefon (telephone → te-le-fon)
```

#### **Ontological Vectors (VCV+CV+...)**

```
apa-ma-ři-gu
ibo-di-na-ke-fo
```

---

### **Consonant Clusters**

**Native words:** Generally avoid clusters.

**Borrowed words:** Clusters are adapted to CV structure.

**Examples:**

|Original|Adapted|Method|
|---|---|---|
|"sport"|`soporu` or `espo`|Insert vowels or simplify|
|"text"|`tekise`|Insert vowels|
|"start"|`setare`|Insert vowels|

**Acceptable native clusters (limited):**

- Nasal + homorganic stop: `mb`, `nd`, `ŋg` (same place of articulation)
- Liquid + consonant: `lp`, `rt`, `rk` (in careful speech, optional vowel insertion)

## 🔄 Allophony

**Allowed pronunciation variations — sounds that can vary without changing meaning.**

### **Principle: Permissive Allophony**

The language **allows natural variation** to accommodate different native language backgrounds.

---

### **/r/ — Rhotic (Most Flexible)**

**Acceptable realizations:**

- [r] — Trilled /r/ (Spanish, Italian, Russian)
- [ɾ] — Tapped /r/ (Portuguese, Japanese)
- [ɹ] — Approximant /r/ (English, Mandarin)
- [ʁ] — Uvular /r/ (French, German)

**All count as the same phoneme /r/.**

**Why?**  
Rhotic sounds vary enormously across languages. Forcing one variant would disadvantage millions of speakers.

---

### **/l/ — Lateral**

**Acceptable realizations:**

- [l] — Clear /l/ (Spanish, Mandarin)
- [ɫ] — Dark /l/ (English, Russian)

**Both acceptable.**

---

### **/h/ — Glottal Fricative**

**Acceptable realizations:**

- [h] — Voiceless glottal fricative (English, Arabic)
- [ɦ] — Voiced glottal fricative (Czech, Ukrainian)
- [x] — Can substitute /x/ (velar) if /h/ is difficult

**Distinction:** /h/ vs. /x/ should be maintained when possible, but overlap is acceptable in casual speech.

---

### **Vowel Length**

**Default:** All vowels are **short** (monomoraic).

**Allowed variation:**

- Vowels can be slightly lengthened for emphasis
- Word-final vowels may be naturally longer (okay)

**NOT phonemic:**  
Long vs. short vowels do **not** change meaning (unlike Japanese or Latin).

---

### **Stress/Accent**

**Default:** **Penultimate stress** (stress on second-to-last syllable)

**Examples:**

- `kafe` → **ka**-fe
- `pitsa` → **pi**-tsa
- `telefon` → te-**le**-fon (middle syllable)

**Exception:** Monosyllables have stress by default.

**Flexible:** Stress can shift for emphasis without changing lexical meaning.

### **Typing Solutions**

**Option 1: Use special characters directly**

- Windows: Alt codes or Character Map
- Mac: Option key combinations
- Linux: Compose key
- Mobile: Long-press on base letter

**Option 2: Use digraphs (two-letter combinations)**

- More accessible for typing
- Slightly longer
- Example: `shano` instead of `šano`

**Both are acceptable.** The community can standardize based on usage.

---

### **Capitalization**

**Standard rules:**

- Proper nouns: Capitalize first letter (`Kafe` for a place name)
- Sentence-initial: Capitalize first word
- Otherwise: lowercase

**No special capitalization rules for grammatical function.**

## 🌍 Pronunciation Guide by Native Language

**Tailored tips for speakers of major languages.**

---

### **For English Speakers**

**Easy sounds (you already know):**

- Most consonants: p, b, t, d, k, g, m, n, f, v, s, z, h, l, w, y
- Vowels: a (as in "father"), i (as in "see"), u (as in "food")

**Watch out for:**

- **/r/** — Use a tapped [ɾ] (like Spanish "pero"), not English [ɹ]
    - _Practice:_ Say "butter" quickly — the "tt" sound is close
- **/e/, /o/** — Don't diphthongize! Keep them pure
    - ❌ "say" [seɪ] → ✅ "se" [se] (like Spanish "que")
    - ❌ "go" [goʊ] → ✅ "go" [go] (like Spanish "lo")
- **/x/** — Back-of-throat friction (like German "Bach" or Scottish "loch")

---

### **For Spanish Speakers**

**Easy sounds (almost everything!):**

- This phonology is very close to Spanish

**Watch out for:**

- **/v/** vs. **/b/** — **Must distinguish!**
    - Spanish often merges these → practice keeping them separate
    - /v/ = teeth on lower lip (like English "very")
    - /b/ = both lips (like Spanish "bueno")
- **/ʃ/ (š)** — Like English "sh", NOT Spanish "ch"
- **/ŋ/ (ň)** — Like English "sing" final sound, not "n+g" (banco)

---

### **For Portuguese Speakers**

**Easy sounds (nearly all!):**

- Portuguese has a rich phonology, almost perfect overlap

**Watch out for:**

- **/r/** — Use tapped [ɾ], not the guttural carioca [ʁ] or [x]
    - Avoid "carro" [kaʁu] → use "caro" [kaɾu] style
- Nasalized vowels — **DON'T nasalize!**
    - ❌ "mãe" → ✅ "ma-e" (separate oral vowels)

---

### **For Mandarin Speakers**

**Easy sounds:**

- p, t, k, m, n, l, f, s, w, y
- Vowels: a, e, i, o, u

**Watch out for:**

- **/r/** — Use an approximant [ɹ] (like English), NOT retroflex [ʐ]
- **/v/** — New sound! Teeth touch lower lip, vibrate vocal cords
    - Practice: Start with /f/, then add voice
- **/b/, /d/, /g/** — Fully voiced, not just unaspirated
    - 不 (bù) vs. 普 (pǔ) — /b/ in this language = 不 style
- **/l/ vs. /r/** — Must distinguish (Mandarin has both but different articulations)

---

### **For Arabic Speakers**

**Easy sounds:**

- Most consonants, especially: b, t, k, m, n, f, s, h, w, y
- /x/ — Exactly like خ

**Watch out for:**

- **/p/** — New sound! Like /b/ but voiceless (no vocal cord vibration)
    - Practice: Whisper "ba" → you get "pa"
- **/v/** — New sound! Like /f/ but voiced
- No emphatic consonants — all sounds are plain (no /tˤ/, /sˤ/, etc.)
- No pharyngeals — /h/ is simple glottal, not /ħ/ or /ʕ/

---

### **For Japanese Speakers**

**Easy sounds:**

- p, t, k, m, n, h, w, y, s, z
- Vowels: a, i, u, e, o (same system!)

**Watch out for:**

- **/r/** vs. **/l/** — **Must distinguish!**
    - Japanese [ɾ] = acceptable for /r/
    - But /l/ is separate — tongue stays on roof of mouth
- **/f/** — Not bilabial [ɸ], but labiodental (upper teeth on lower lip)
- **/v/** — New sound! Like /f/ but voiced
- **/si/** vs. **/ši/** — Don't merge!
    - し (shi) = /ši/
    - /si/ is separate (like "see")

---

### **For Russian Speakers**

**Easy sounds (Russian has rich phonology):**

- Almost everything, especially palatalized consonants map well

**Watch out for:**

- No palatalization distinction — /n/ and /nʲ/ both → /n/
    - Exception: /ɲ/ is a separate phoneme (like Spanish "ñ")
- **/h/** — Simpler than Russian /x/, more like English "h"
- Vowels — No reduction in unstressed syllables
    - ❌ [ə] in unstressed positions → ✅ Full vowels always

## ✅ Key Takeaways

### **For Learners:**

1. **30 sounds total** — manageable inventory
2. **Most sounds you already know** — from your native language or English
3. **CV syllable structure** — simple and clear
4. **Flexible pronunciation** — /r/ especially has many acceptable variants
5. **No tones, no pitch accent** — stress is simple and predictable

### **For Linguists:**

1. **Typologically balanced** — covers all major manners and places
2. **Cross-linguistically accessible** — sounds from IPA Top 25
3. **Phonotactically simple** — CV preference, minimal clusters
4. **Allophonically permissive** — accommodates L2 variation
5. **Orthographically practical** — Latin script with minimal diacritics
